What rideshare driving taught me about communication

July 12, 2025 • Life Lessons

After thousands of rides, I’ve picked up more than just passengers. Here’s how driving strangers around Austin shaped my approach to listening, empathy and conversation.

I used to think small talk was pointless – until I realised it’s an art. Now I treat each ride like an improv scene. I mirror my rider’s energy, ask open questions and build rapport quickly. Being neurodivergent, this required practice and systems: I keep a deck of mental prompts (favourite breakfast? best video game?), practise active listening by repeating back what I hear, and allow silence when needed.

Communicating isn’t always easy when your brain runs on high speed, but rideshare has taught me to slow down. I’ve learned to focus on one conversation at a time, use humour to defuse tension and respect boundaries. And when all else fails, I let the music or the dog videos I keep on my tablet do the talking!

Top support role tips for Smite

June 30, 2025 • Gaming

Playing support in Smite is like being the team’s unsung hero. You facilitate kills, set up vision and protect your carries. Here are my favourite tips to level up your support game:

  • Ward everything: Vision wins games. Prioritise warding objectives and enemy jungle paths.
  • Peel for your carries: Body‑block skillshots and use crowd control to keep your damage dealers alive.
  • Communicate rotations: Use pings and voice chat to coordinate ganks and lane swaps.
  • Build tanky but smart: Adapt your items to counter enemy threats while boosting your team’s survivability.

Remember, playing support isn’t about chasing kills – it’s about enabling your teammates. Once you embrace that mindset, you’ll start seeing bigger wins and better teamwork. See you on the battlefield!

Building an ADHD‑friendly habit tracker

May 18, 2025 • Self‑Care

Keeping track of habits can feel overwhelming when your brain rejects rigid routines. I experimented with dozens of systems before finding what works for me. Here’s how to build a habit tracker that adapts to ADHD brains:

  • Keep it visual: Use colours, stickers and icons instead of plain checkboxes. Visual cues make the process fun and rewarding.
  • Make tasks bite‑sized: Break down big habits into micro‑actions. “Clean room” becomes “pick up one item”.
  • Use positive reinforcement: Celebrate small wins with a reward or a note of encouragement. Don’t punish lapses.
  • Allow flexibility: Don’t aim for perfect streaks; accept that some days will be chaotic. Track patterns rather than perfection.

I’ve created printable templates and a simple digital spreadsheet that you can adapt. Download them from the toolkit section and tweak colours, prompts and categories to suit your needs.
